You won’t believe the nearly billion-dollar twist in Donald Trump’s long-running feud with wind power: the U.S. government just agreed to pay France’s TotalEnergies a stunning 928 million dollars to walk away from two offshore wind farms off New Jersey and North Carolina. Picture it as a high-stakes energy breakup — two projects that could have powered about 1.7 million homes are being told thanks, but no thanks.

In a move that has salons buzzing and policy wonks gasping, the Trump administration framed the payout as a savvy correction, with Interior Secretary Doug Burgum blasting offshore wind as costly, unreliable, and propped up by subsidies. The message: ditch the turbines, take the check, and refocus on U.S. fossil fuel investments. It is an eye-popping use of taxpayer money that signals a hardened stance against America’s nascent offshore wind sector.

{{< articlead >}}

Here’s the tea: this appears to be a novel lever — paying a global energy giant not to build clean power — and it dovetails with a broader White House push to slow or stop offshore wind build-out. Environmentalists are calling it shortsighted and bad for consumers, arguing that while offshore wind still costs more than many alternatives, its price tag has been tumbling for years. According to a 2024 U.S. Department of Energy report, global offshore wind costs have dropped more than 50 percent since 2013 and are expected to keep sliding as technology improves and supply chains mature. In other words, critics say the industry is finally hitting its stride just as the rug is being tugged.

From the boardroom to the boatyard, the implications are juicy. States like New Jersey and North Carolina have been sketching out ports, grid upgrades, and local supply chains in anticipation of offshore projects. Halting two marquee builds could ripple through those plans, hitting manufacturers, port authorities, and union workforce pipelines. Investors, meanwhile, just got a masterclass in federal-policy whiplash: if a project can be paid to vanish, companies may hedge their bets, demand firmer guarantees, or angle for make-good deals before committing billions.

Politically, the optics are pure primetime. Supporters of the deal will tout it as protecting ratepayers and restoring focus to domestic oil and gas. Detractors will call it an expensive detour that sends almost a billion public dollars overseas while undercutting long-term climate and energy-security goals. TotalEnergies gets to pivot with a golden parachute; the administration gets a headline-grabbing proof point in its anti-wind agenda. And the rest of us? We’re left watching the breeze die down where turbines were supposed to spin, wondering if this blockbuster cancellation is a one-off drama or the pilot episode of a much longer series.
