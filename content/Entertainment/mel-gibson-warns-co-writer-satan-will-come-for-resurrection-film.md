Before a single page of the long-awaited 'Passion of the Christ' sequel was written, Mel Gibson delivered a bone-chilling spiritual warning to his collaborator: 'Satan's going to come after you.' This bombshell revelation comes from Oscar-winning screenwriter Randall Wallace, who co-wrote 'The Resurrection of the Christ' with the famously intense director. In a conversation dripping with more drama than a biblical epic, Wallace recounted pitching the idea to Gibson while promoting 'Hacksaw Ridge,' only to be met with a profound and ominous pause.

Gibson, whose own career has been a rollercoaster of controversy and redemption, laid down the divine law. Wallace recalled Gibson insisting the project could not be motivated by money or revenge against their detractors. But it was the dire prediction of demonic opposition that truly set the tone. Wallace, playing it off with a Baptist's bravado, joked that 'Satan doesn't really care about Baptists,' but later admitted to the Fox News podcast host, 'What if he's right?' The implication is clear: making this film is a spiritual battleground.

The duo, along with Gibson's brother Donal, spent six to eight years laboring over the screenplay, a period one can only imagine was fraught with metaphysical tension. Now, with production finally wrapped and a two-part release scheduled for 2027 and 2028, the question isn't just whether it will match the original's staggering $610 million box office—it's whether the team has spiritually armored up for the backlash they anticipate. Gibson calls it 'a mission I've carried for over 20 years,' suggesting this is more than a movie; it's a holy war waged on celluloid.

Wallace teased that Gibson's 'unique vision of the spiritual realm' will deliver scenes 'nobody's ever seen,' promising a film that is both terrifyingly original and fiercely orthodox. It's the ultimate Hollywood paradox: a project expecting divine inspiration and diabolical sabotage in equal measure. As the release dates loom, one has to wonder: will the lines at the box office be matched by unseen lines of spiritual conflict? Gibson seems to think so. Buckle up, believers and skeptics alike; this comeback story comes with a celestial warning label.
